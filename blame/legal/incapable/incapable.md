# [blame](../../blame.md)/legal/incapable/incapable

The most significant provisions are the ones that define "incapable of managing property" and then automatically transfer or terminate Eliot's control rights if that threshold is met.

**Key concern:**

* The trust defines "incapable of managing property" as either:
  * a court declaration, **or**
  * written statements from **two physicians licensed in Canada**.

That definition is broad enough that a finding tied to mania or mental incapacity could trigger loss of powers under the trust.

The practical consequences appear in several places:

1. **Loss of trustee appointment power**  
   If Eliot is found "incapable of managing property," his power to appoint additional or substitute trustees shifts away from him. Initially it transfers to Leanne alone; failing that, it can pass to the children or remaining trustees.

2. **Loss of trustee removal power**  
   If Eliot is deemed incapable, he loses the unilateral/joint ability to remove trustees without cause. That authority can move to Leanne alone or later to a majority of trustees.

3. **Automatic termination as trustee**  
   If Eliot is serving as a trustee, the office is automatically terminated if he is "found to be incapable of managing property."

4. **Loss of beneficiary-control powers**  
   Clauses 9(j) and 9(k) give Eliot and Leanne powers to add or exclude beneficiaries, but those powers only exist while Eliot is "alive and not incapable of managing property."

In short, the structural issue is not the word "insane" specifically — it is the trust's use of the legal concept "incapable of managing property," combined with a relatively accessible evidentiary threshold (two physicians), which can shift governance and control rights away from Eliot.
